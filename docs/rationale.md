# Rationale

Software engineers who move into lead roles lose the feedback loops that made them good. Code compiles or it does not. Tests pass or they fail. Whether you just answered a question someone needed to work through themselves produces no signal. Neither does staying silent when you see a risk nobody named. Without correction, the defaults win.

## Why Katas

Dave Thomas introduced Code Katas in 2003 as a way to give developers what musicians and athletes already had: a short, repeatable exercise designed to build fluency through repetition. Mike Rother brought the same format to management in _Toyota Kata_. Ted Neward adapted it for software architecture. The format has been in active use in the software industry for over 20 years.

The idea is the same across all of them: focused, repeated practice of a specific behavior. A kata that says "communicate more clearly" gives you nothing to do in the moment. A kata that says "write 3 sentences before you send it, remove any word they would need to look up" gives you something to do right now. The specificity is the point.

Each kata in this catalogue targets one behavior, attaches it to one triggering moment, and asks you to practice it when that moment arrives.

## Why Real Work

Most leadership development happens away from the work: workshops, courses, books. People engage with the material, then return to their jobs and behave largely as before. The gap between learning and doing is where development disappears.

Lacerenza et al. studied 335 leadership training programs and found that practice-based methods and immediate application were among the strongest predictors of success. Hughes et al. found that peer support at work accounts for more of the variance in whether training sticks than either supervisor support or organizational support.

The katas are designed to run inside your actual work, not alongside it. The trigger is a moment you are already in. The behavior is narrow enough to focus on deliberately. The kata constrains the action, not the situation.

A colleague practicing alongside you is part of the design. This is why the format is built around a practice partner.

## Why These Behaviors

Patrick Kua interviewed more than 35 tech leads for _Talking with Tech Leads_. The same pattern surfaces throughout: engineers in leadership roles invest disproportionately in technical skills because those are familiar, and systematically underinvest in people and process skills. Not because they lack the capacity, but because the environment does not develop those skills the way it develops technical ones.

Gergely Orosz makes a related point in _The Software Engineer's Guidebook_. The tech lead role requires risk identification, stakeholder management, and distributing decisions instead of making them yourself. A common failure mode is assuming that when a decision needs to be made, you should make it.

Without signal from the environment, engineers default to what worked before. They code when they should delegate. They answer when they should ask. They avoid conflict because the cost of raising it is immediate and visible, while the cost of silence is diffuse and delayed.

Each kata names one of these defaults and specifies the alternative behavior. The closing check is a prompt to notice what happened. It is not a substitute for external feedback. It is more than the environment provides on its own.

## Why Fixed Steps

The steps in each kata do not change. This is a deliberate choice.

Mike Rother's work on Toyota's management practices found that fixed behavioral routines, repeated until they become habitual, produced more durable change than ad hoc responses. The steps do not change so that the cognitive overhead of deciding how to respond in the moment is removed. You execute the steps.

Wallo, Lundqvist, and Coetzer's review of learning-oriented leadership found that the leaders who most effectively developed their teams were not the ones who taught the most. They were the ones who structured work so that learning happened as a byproduct of doing.

General principles require judgment at the moment of application. Fixed steps give you something specific to do instead. Over time, the steps become the response.
